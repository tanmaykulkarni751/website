Can Justice Exist Between Humans and Animals? An Aristotelian Analysis.

In this paper, I will demonstrate that justice towards animals exists within the Aristotelian framework. I will do this by analyzing the reasoning behind Aristotle’s rejection of the notion that justice can exist towards animals and critiquing the arguments he offers to support his position.

If I am successful in my endeavor, the manner in which we discuss morality must be altered. Particularly, the fact that animals are capable of being treated (un)justly will have to be taken into consideration when discussing ethics; animals will have to be granted moral consideration. For the consequentialist, this means that the interests of animals would have to be accounted for in the calculus that determines the moral course of action. On the other hand, for the non-consequentialist, this means that there would exist reasons, exclusive of the consequences of an action, to act such that the interests of non-human animals are upheld. As a result, we would have to seriously re-evaluate our current treatment of non-human animals. That is, we ought to scrutinize the manner in which we confine animals in factory farms wherein they experience acute amounts of pain throughout their short lives, but we would also have to re-evaluate how we view them in general. I hope that this paper also highlights the shaky grounds upon which consuming animal flesh and secretions stands.

A brief roadmap. This paper is divided into five sections. In the first section, I will provide an overview of Aristotle’s position and two primary arguments he offers in support of it. The second and third sections are dedicated to addressing the first argument Aristotle offers, while the fourth section addresses the second argument he offers in support of the impossibility of the existence of justice towards animals. The final section is a brief conclusion.

Section I. Introduction

Aristotle presents his conception of justice in relation to the existence of injustice. He writes that “justice exists only between men whose mutual relations are governed by law; and law exists for men between whom there is injustice.” That is, justice exists only insofar as injustice exists. Thus, in order to show that justice towards animals can exist, it would suffice to show that injustice towards animals can exist.

We claim that an injustice has occurred when one has been wronged. For instance, stealing someone’s property is unjust because the person whose property was stolen has been wronged. Justice to the victim, in this instance, could then (at the very least) be the undoing of the unjust action, i.e., the restoration of the stolen property. Thus, for justice to exist towards animals, it must be that animals are capable of being wronged by humans (more on this in Section V).

I have identified two chief arguments Aristotle offers to support the claim that justice towards animals cannot exist. His first argument is that because animals cannot form social contracts with humans, justice towards them cannot exist. I will address this argument in the following two sections. His second argument stems from the claim that one cannot wrong an entity over which one has ownership. Aristotle believes that humans have ownership over animals, and therefore, animals cannot be wronged by humans. So, justice towards animals cannot exist. I will address this argument in the penultimate two sections of this paper.

Section II. Aristotle’s First Argument

Aristotle holds that animals are natural slaves of human beings because “there is nothing common” between humans and animals. The commonality he refers to, or lack thereof, is an ability to “be party to an agreement,” i.e., an ability to form a social contract. Thus, according to Aristotle, since a social contract cannot exist between humans and animals, justice towards animals cannot exist. 

        The form of this argument can be formally represented as follows:
          1. If x can be (un)just to y, a social contract can exist between x and y 
          2. A social contract cannot exist between x and y 
          3. Therefore, x cannot be (un)just to y

In addition, Aristotle asserts that animals are incapable of rational thought. Sorabji notes that in rejecting the notion that animals are capable of reason, Aristotle denies that they are capable of having true or false beliefs. Thus, Aristotle posits that animals must therefore be incapable of forming social contracts because doing so presupposes having a belief that the contract is good for themselves. Accordingly, since a social contract cannot exist between humans and animals due to the fact that animals are incapable of reason, justice towards animals cannot exist.

Singer’s argument from marginal cases is a direct response to premise 1 of the above argument. Since Aristotle posits that the ability to form a social contract (and therefore a capacity for rationality) is a requirement for one to be treated unjustly, a severely cognitively impaired child whose capacity for rationality does not exceed that of a pig cannot be treated unjustly. As a result, holding the impaired child in isolation within pens that restrict any possibility of movement and slaughtering them once they have grown to an appropriate size would be non-problematic.

Although this implication opposes the ethical intuitions of perhaps even the most obstinate speciesist, I find that Aristotle would be compelled to accept it. This is not only because rejecting the implication would compromise logical consistency but also because Aristotle would not consider individuals who are incapable of reason as members of the human species. Aristotle defines a ‘human being’ as a ‘rational animal.’ Accordingly, any animal that lacks the capacity for rationality would, by definition, not be considered to be a human being. Thus, in Aristotle’s worldview, animals, as well as severely cognitively impaired individuals, would not deserve moral consideration. I imagine, however, that most ethically serious individuals would be compelled to reject premise 1 of Aristotle’s argument upon learning of its implications.

Section III.

Another line of argumentation against Aristotle’s reasoning questions his support for the premise that animals cannot form social contracts (premise 2). In particular, Aristotle’s rationale that since animals are incapable of reason, they cannot form social contracts can be critiqued.

In his seminal On Abstinence from Killing Animals, Porphyry dedicates a large portion of the text to show that animals are capable of reasoning. He fundamentally challenges the dichotomy made by Aristotle between rational and non-rational when positing a difference in kind between humans and animals. That is, he argues against Aristotle that the difference in rationality between humans and animals is not one of kind, but one of degree.

One of the arguments Porphyry offers against the notion that animals are incapable of reason goes as follows. He writes that much like the existence of the pair immortal and mortal, if the rational exists, “the irrational must exist as its opposite.” The irrational, he argues, exists and is “important and abundant” in everything that lacks a soul. Moreover, just as how it is absurd to create a dichotomy between sentient and non-sentient aspects or aspects with and without impression in animate creatures, “it is also not plausible to require that an animate creature should have a rational and an irrational aspect.”

Although Aristotle denies reason to animals altogether in several texts , he admits in one instance that animals are capable of practical wisdom (phronesis). Specifically, Aristotle admits that some animals possess “a power of foresight with regard to their own life.” This hypothesis explains why animals choose a particular end – ingesting water to quench thirst, for instance – as well as the means they employ to achieve the end – drinking the water. Nevertheless, he remains committed to the view that only “the human race lives also by art and reasonings.”

Porphyry criticizes the methodology in which Aristotle reaches this conclusion. He writes: 

        If we do not understand how an animal acts because we cannot enter 
        into their reasoning, we shall not therefore accuse them of 
        non-rationality. No one can enter the intellect of God, but from 
        the acts of the sun we agree with those who proclaim him to be 
        intellectual and rational.

Here, Porphyry touches upon the fact that Aristotle begins with the assumption that animals are incapable of reason and attempts to formulate a post hoc rationalization to explain any evidence that supports the existence of a capacity to reason within animals. This methodology is problematic; particularly, given the impossibility of entering into the minds of animals, why must the starting point be non-rationality and not rationality?

I am aware that these arguments do not demonstrate that animals are capable of forming social contracts. However, they succeed in revealing the dubiousness of the claim that because animals cannot reason, they cannot form social contracts. Furthermore, some studies have shown that animals are, in fact, capable of forming non-verbal social contracts with humans. Thus, since there exists, at the very least, uncertainty about the claim that animals cannot reason, there must also exist uncertainty about the claim that animals cannot form social contracts. In particular, one would have to provide separate argumentation in order to show that animals cannot form social contracts (the burden for which lies on the Aristotelian). Hence, we have arrived (at least) at a position of agnosticism regarding whether animals can form social contracts, revealing that Aristotle’s argument for why justice towards animals cannot exist remains unsubstantiated.

Section III. Aristotle’s Second Argument

Another support Aristotle offers for the impossibility of justice towards animals is rooted in his belief that animals exist “for the sake of man.” That is, since animals belong to man, it must be impossible to wrong them. Accordingly, the absence of a possibility to wrong animals implies the absence of a possibility to extend (in)justice towards animals.

While Aristotle does not offer direct argumentation for the claim that since animals belong to humans, it is impossible to wrong them, we can infer this position from his discussion of justice towards one’s slaves and one’s women and children. One might argue that animals differ from slaves, women, and children in that they possess different degrees of rationality. This difference subsequently reveals that Aristotle’s position on the presence of justice towards the latter cannot be applied to animals.

Although Aristotle indeed recognizes that – unlike animals – slaves, women, and children “share in rational principle,” he believes that like animals, they lack a proper faculty of deliberation. On this, he writes: “the slave has no deliberative faculty at all; the woman has it, but it is without authority; and the child has, but it is immature.” As a result, Aristotle declares that “the use of slaves and of tame animals is not very different.”

Aristotle holds that the virtues of men and those of slaves, women, and children are not the same. The virtues of women, for instance, are only proportionate to their duties and do not exceed that of the man. According to Aristotle, because men have command over women, “the courage of a man is shown in commanding, of a woman in obeying.” Therefore, since the virtues of a man greatly exceed the virtues of those he commands, it would be impossible for man to wrong them. The same reasoning applies to non-human animals.

The above position also stems from the belief that you cannot wrong an entity that is entirely your own. For example, if I put my foot in harm’s way, it cannot be said that I have wronged my foot. A more precise description would be that I have wronged myself since my foot belongs to me. However, Aristotle provides little argumentation to support his belief that slaves, women, children, and non-human animals belong to man. He assumes that they belong to man as a starting point and builds his theories from there. Thus, his belief that justice towards animals cannot exist is based on an unsubstantiated premise.

From this discussion, I want to highlight that the issues of justice commonplace today – racism, sexism, and speciesism, for instance – are quite related. Such positions that deny moral consideration to someone stem from an unsupported dogmatic belief. The burden of proof thus lies on the ones who deny moral consideration, and until this burden is resolved, the default position should be one wherein moral consideration is not denied.

Section IV. Animals Can Be Wronged

In this final section, I will demonstrate that animals are indeed capable of being wronged. In his work Topica, Aristotle defines ‘wronging’ as “to injure deliberately.” Consider this example: a man picks up a club and hits a stray animal several times because he derives pleasure from viewing the animal’s injuries. It is trivially apparent that the man has deliberately injured the animal. Therefore, by Aristotle’s definition of wronging, the man has wronged the animal.

In this example, since a human being has wronged an animal, we infer that it must be possible for humans to wrong animals. Thus, this example refutes the claim that it is impossible to wrong animals. Furthermore, since it is possible to wrong animals, the Aristotelian must concede that justice towards animals exists.

One might argue that by consuming meat, dairy, and eggs, they are not committing an injustice towards animals because although the animals bred for these products do suffer injury, the consumer does not purchase the products with the intention of causing injury. By consuming animal products, one is not deliberately injuring animals. Consequently, they are not committing an injustice towards the animals. This line of reasoning perfectly serves as an Aristotelian argument against veganism. However, I find that this says less about the moral status of non-human animals and more about the inadequacy of Aristotle’s definition for wronging. For instance, if one accepts this definition, they are committed to the view that most instances of rape are just because the rapist rapes not with a deliberate intention of causing injury but only for the pleasure they derive from the act. If the injury did not exist, they would still rape. However, if this pleasure did not exist, they would not rape. Any ethically serious individual would reject this implication, and therefore the argument that consuming animal flesh and secretions is just since the consumer’s intention is not to injure deliberately.

Nevertheless, Aristotle’s definition for wronging is not entirely useless. Whether an actor has caused injury to a victim deliberately or not is helpful in determining the degree of their moral responsibility. However, since issues of justice are viewed from the victim’s perspective, the pig that suffers in a gas chamber is wronged regardless of the degree to which the perpetrator’s actions were deliberate. Any apt definition for ‘wronging’ should capture this idea. Since Aristotle’s definition of the term is too narrow, a good definition would likely be a superset of Aristotle’s definition; as a result, the argument I have offered for the existence of justice towards animals would likely hold even for a different definition. What exactly serves as a good definition for the term lies outside the scope of this paper, however.

Section V. Conclusion

In this paper, I hope to have done the following things. Firstly, by critiquing Aristotle’s support for the impossibility of justice towards animals, I hope to have demonstrated that justice towards animals can exist within the Aristotelian framework. Secondly, I hope to have shown that the arguments I have offered do not fail outside the Aristotelian framework. This is because the grounds upon which the exploitation of animals is maintained are similar across most major ethical frameworks, and thus, the arguments I have offered should extend and apply within other frameworks as well.
